1. INTRODUCTION TO FUNGI

Key Terminology

  • Mycology: The study of fungi
  • Mycologists: Scientists who study fungi
  • Mycosis: Disease caused by fungi
  • Mycotoxicology: Study of fungal toxins and their effects
  • Mycota/Mycetes: Terms used to describe fungal groups (interchangeable)
  • Mycelium: The thread-like structures that form the fungal body

General Characteristics

  • Eukaryotic organisms: Contain membrane-bound organelles (unlike bacteria)
  • Cell wall composition: Made of chitin (polymer of N-acetylglucosamine) and β-1,3 and β-1,6 glucans
  • Cell membrane: Contains ergosterol (not cholesterol like animals) - important drug target
  • Nutrition: Osmotrophic - absorb nutrients through entire surface
  • Storage carbohydrate: Glycogen (like animals, not starch)
  • Distribution: Terrestrial organisms with global distribution
  • Diversity: 2-5 million estimated species; ~5,000+ are pathogenic

2. MAJOR FUNGAL GROUPS

Group 1: ZOOSPORIC FUNGI

A. Microsporidia

Characteristics:
  • Obligate intracellular parasites
  • No free-living forms
  • Lack mitochondria, peroxisomes, centrioles
  • Polar tube for host penetration
  • Reduced genome size

B. Chytridiomycota

⚠️ AMPHIBIAN CRISIS

  • Causes skin thickening (hyperkeratinization)
  • Impedes gas exchange through skin
  • Blood electrolyte imbalance → cardiac arrest → death
  • Threatening global amphibian extinction

Group 2: ZYGOMYCOTA

A. Mucormycota - Rhizopus stolonifer

Structure:

  • Rhizoids: Absorb nutrients from substrate
  • Stolon: Horizontal hyphae
  • Sporangiophore: Stalk supporting sporangium
  • Sporangium: Sac containing spores
Image 3: Rhizopus Life Cycle - Zygomycota

ASEXUAL REPRODUCTION (Top of Image 3):

  1. Spores germinate on substrate (bread)
  2. Rhizoids penetrate and absorb nutrients
  3. Stolons grow horizontally
  4. Sporangiophores grow vertically
  5. Sporangium forms at tip containing haploid spores (n)
  6. Sporangium bursts, releasing spores
  7. Cycle repeats

SEXUAL REPRODUCTION (Bottom of Image 3 - Unfavorable conditions):

  1. PLASMOGAMY: (+) and (-) mating strains meet via pheromones
  2. Hyphae fuse, cytoplasm combines
  3. Gametangia form - thick-walled structures
  4. Dikaryotic stage (n+n): Two nuclei remain separate
  5. KARYOGAMY: Nuclear fusion → zygosporangium (2n)
  6. Zygospore forms - resistant to adverse conditions
  7. MEIOSIS: When favorable, reduction division occurs
  8. Haploid spores (n) released, germinate into new thallus

Commercial Importance:

  • Tempeh production (fermented soybeans)
  • Sufu curd (fermented tofu)
  • Produces alcohols, meat tenderizers
  • Yellow coloring for margarine

B. Glomeromycotina (Mycorrhizae)

  • Form arbuscular endomycorrhizae with vascular plants
  • Mutualistic: fungus provides nutrients, plant provides carbohydrates
  • Essential for plant survival
  • Appressoria penetrate plant roots

Group 3: DIKARYA

Named for dikaryotic stage where two nuclei (n+n) remain separate after cytoplasmic fusion.

Key Feature: Cell membranes contain ergosterol (not cholesterol) - excellent antifungal drug target!
Image 4: General Fungal Sexual Reproduction Cycle

COMPLETE SEXUAL REPRODUCTION CYCLE (Image 4):

Stage 1: HAPLOID STAGE (n) - Green section (LARGEST)

  • Dominant stage - most time spent here
  • Single set of chromosomes
  • Asexual reproduction via mitosis and spores
  • Vegetative growth

Stage 2: CYTOPLASMIC FUSION (Plasmogamy)

  • Two compatible haploid cells meet
  • Cytoplasm fuses
  • Nuclei do NOT fuse yet!

Stage 3: DIKARYOTIC STAGE (n+n) - Pink section

  • UNIQUE TO FUNGI
  • Two separate haploid nuclei in same cell
  • Can persist for hours, days, or weeks
  • Critical for fungal life cycle

Stage 4: NUCLEAR FUSION (Karyogamy) - Yellow oval

  • Nuclei finally fuse: n + n → 2n
  • Forms true diploid zygote
  • Occurs in specific structures (ascus, basidium, zygosporangium)

Stage 5: DIPLOID STAGE (2n) - Blue section (SMALLEST)

  • Shortest stage in most fungi
  • Two complete chromosome sets
  • Quickly proceeds to meiosis

Stage 6: MEIOSIS - Yellow oval

  • Meiosis I: 2n → n (reduction division - 2 haploid nuclei)
  • Meiosis II: n → n (mitotic division - 4 haploid nuclei)
  • Sometimes additional mitosis → 8-12 spores
  • Results in haploid spores

Stage 7: RETURN TO HAPLOID

  • Spores germinate
  • New haploid organism grows
  • Cycle begins again
Key Insight: Fungi are haploid-dominant (most time as n). Animals are diploid-dominant (most time as 2n). The dikaryotic stage is a unique fungal adaptation!

A. ASCOMYCOTA (Sac Fungi)

Named for the ascus (sac) containing ascospores. Largest fungal group.

Characteristics:

  • Septate hyphae (have cross-walls with pores)
  • Dimorphic: yeast or mycelial forms
  • Ascus typically contains 8 ascospores
  • Brightly colored (red, brown, blue, green)

Important Structures:

  • Ascus: Sac containing ascospores
  • Ascocarp: Cup-like fruiting body
  • Ascogonium: Female reproductive hypha
  • Antheridium: Male reproductive hypha
  • Conidiophore: Stalk bearing conidia
  • Conidia: Asexual spores in chains
Image 2: Ascomycota Life Cycle

ASEXUAL REPRODUCTION (Top right of Image 2):

  1. Haploid (n) ascospores germinate
  2. Developing mycelium with septate hyphae forms
  3. Conidiophores develop (specialized aerial hyphae)
  4. Conidia form in chains on conidiophores
  5. Conidia = "naked spores" (not in sac)
  6. Conidia disperse and germinate
  7. Mitosis produces new mycelium

SEXUAL REPRODUCTION (Bottom of Image 2):

  1. GERMINATION: Ascospores germinate
  2. Compatible mating strains (+ and -) grow separately
  3. Each forms septate mycelium
  4. FERTILIZATION: Ascogonium (-) meets Antheridium (+)
  5. Pheromone secretion attracts compatible types
  6. Plasmogamy occurs (cytoplasmic fusion)
  7. DIKARYOTIC HYPHAE: Ascogenous hyphae grow (n+n nuclei separate)
  8. ASCOCARP FORMATION: Cup-shaped fruiting body develops
  9. Contains dikaryotic (ascogenic) + sterile hyphae
  10. YOUNG ASCUS: Still dikaryotic (n+n), nuclei not fused
  11. KARYOGAMY: Nuclear fusion → diploid (2n) zygote
  12. MEIOSIS:
    • First division: 2n → n (2 haploid nuclei)
    • Second division (mitosis): n → n (4 haploid nuclei)
    • Additional mitosis: 4 → 8 ascospores
  13. MATURE ASCUS: Contains 8 haploid ascospores
  14. Ascospores released, cycle repeats

Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Baker's Yeast)

Yeast Form:

  • Reproduces by budding at unscarred regions
  • When entire surface scarred → senescence (aging) → death

Vegetative Cycle (Nutrient-rich):

  • Haploid ascospores (α or a types) germinate
  • Mitosis and budding produce daughter cells
  • Continues until surface fully scarred

Sexual Reproduction (Nutrient-poor):

  • α meets a mating types
  • Form diploid zygote (2n)
  • Can remain as diploid yeast OR form mycelium
  • When favorable: meiosis → ascus with 4 haploid spores (2α, 2a)
  • Return to vegetative cycle
Research Importance: Model organism for eukaryotic cell biology. Almost all cell cycle control discoveries came from yeast research!

Important Ascomycota Species:

BENEFICIAL:

  • Penicillium: First antibiotic (penicillin) - changed biomedical science
  • Saccharomyces: Baking, wine, beer production
  • Aspergillus oryzae: Sake, soy sauce, miso
  • Neurospora crassa: Research model organism

PATHOGENIC:

  • Aspergillus: Allergies, asthma, sinusitis; fatal in immunocompromised; produces aflatoxins (carcinogenic)
  • Candida albicans: Ringworm, jock itch, athlete's foot, thrush, candidiasis
  • Histoplasma, Blastomyces: Serious subdermal infections
  • Stachybotrys: Black mold, "Sick Building Syndrome"
  • Claviceps purpurea: Ergotism ("Saint Anthony's Fire"), contains LSD, killed thousands in Middle Ages
  • Fusarium: Produces fumonisins (potent mycotoxins)
  • Pseudogymnoascus destructans: White-nose syndrome in bats, threatens extinction by 2026

B. BASIDIOMYCOTA (Club Fungi)

Includes mushrooms, shelf fungi, puffballs, rusts, and smuts. Named for basidium (club-shaped structure).

Characteristics:

  • Many macroscopic (visible to naked eye)
  • Can be very large (1.5-2 feet diameter)
  • Basidium: club-shaped, typically 4 basidiospores
  • Basidiocarp: fruiting body (mushroom)

Mushroom Structure:

  • Cap (top)
  • Gills (underneath, lined with basidia)
  • Stipe (stalk)
  • Basidium: on gill surface
  • Sterigma: projection from basidium
  • Basidiospore: forms at sterigma end

Sexual Reproduction in Mushrooms:

  1. Basidium contains 2 nuclei (dikaryotic, n+n)
  2. Karyogamy: nuclei fuse → diploid (2n)
  3. Meiosis: 2n → 4 haploid nuclei
  4. 4 basidiospores form (haploid, n)
  5. Millions dispersed from gills
  6. Germinate → monokaryotic mycelium (n)
  7. Compatible strains meet and fuse
  8. Secondary mycelium forms (dikaryotic, n+n)
  9. Dikaryotic stage persists through basidiocarp formation
  10. Mushroom emerges
  11. Basidia mature in gills
  12. Karyogamy in basidia
  13. Cycle repeats
Image 1: Basidiomycota Plant Pathogens - Rust and Smut Life Cycle

PLANT PATHOGENS: Rusts and Smuts (Image 1)

Two groups cause devastating plant diseases:

  • Uredinomycetes: Rust diseases
  • Ustilaginomycetes: Smut diseases
  • Both are dimorphic: yeast OR mycelial forms

COMPLETE DISEASE CYCLE (Image 1):

Stage 1: Haploid Sporidia (n) - Yeast Form

  • Single-celled, non-infectious
  • Cannot infect plants in this stage

Stage 2: Conjugation Tube Formation

  • Two compatible sporidia meet
  • Form conjugation tube
  • Cells begin to fuse

Stage 3: Dikaryotic Filament (n+n) - INFECTIOUS FORM

  • After plasmogamy
  • Forms filamentous dikaryotic mycelium
  • Two nuclei separate (n+n)
  • This is the infectious form!

Stage 4: Appressorium Formation

  • Specialized structure on plant surface
  • Flattened, pad-like
  • Attaches firmly to plant

Stage 5: Plant Penetration

  • Appressorium penetrates plant tissue
  • Dikaryotic hyphae invade plant cells
  • Grows deep into plant

Stage 6: Proliferation and Branching

  • Extensive hyphal growth inside plant
  • Septate hyphae spread
  • Forms complex mycelial network
  • Absorbs nutrients from plant

Stage 7: Karyogamy

  • Nuclear fusion: n+n → 2n
  • Forms diploid nuclei
